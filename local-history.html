Local History

Bogoslovskoye-na-Mogiltsakh, and the Ancient Trinity Road

The history of this land spans more than five centuries. The village of Mogiltsy has been documented since the sixteenth century, and after a wooden Church of St. John the Theologian was moved here, it took the name Bogoslovskoye-na-Mogiltsakh.

The great asset of this land was always the ancient Trinity Road — one of Russia’s most important pilgrimage routes, connecting Moscow to the Trinity Lavra of St. Sergius. For centuries, thousands of pilgrims travelled along it. Dmitry Donskoy passed this way to receive the blessing of Sergius of Radonezh before the Battle of Kulikovo; Ivan the Terrible rode through repeatedly; Alexei Mikhailovich and Peter I — the latter fleeing the Streltsy uprising — followed the same road, along with many members of the House of Romanov.

The journey took several days, so travel palaces and estates arose nearby for the rest of royal travellers. Close by stood the palaces of Sofrino and Bratovshchina, where Elizabeth Petrovna and Catherine II once stayed. According to tradition, Emperor Paul I also spent time in Bogoslovskoye during his own pilgrimage to the Lavra.

In the eighteenth century, Pyotr Shcherbachev built a stone Church of St. John the Theologian here, together with a manor house, a park and a system of picturesque ponds. The estate later passed to Prince Gavriil Gagarin, who kept a menagerie of deer on the grounds.

The late nineteenth century is associated with the merchant Vasily Aigin, who restored both the estate and the church. At the same time, the neighbouring estates belonged to the Armand family. It was here, in Yeldigino and the Bogoslovskaya volost, that the public work of Inessa Armand began — one of the most notable women of the early twentieth century, who later became a close associate of Vladimir Lenin. Here she opened a library and a clinic, and helped local peasants through charitable work, long before her name entered Russian history.

A special place among the sights nearby belongs to the holy spring of St. Sergius of Radonezh, known as Gremyachy Klyuch. According to tradition, the spring appeared after Sergius prayed here while travelling through the Radonezh lands. For centuries, pilgrims have come for its pure water, believed to be healing and restorative. Today it remains one of the best-known spiritual and natural landmarks of the Moscow region, surrounded by wooden chapels and centuries-old forest.

Today, Bogoslovskoye-na-Mogiltsakh is a remarkable place where the history of Russian country estates, an ancient pilgrim road, old-growth forest and the cultural memory of several eras all meet. It is this atmosphere of silence, nature and history that makes The Forest Chalet part of a unique historical landscape of the Moscow region.
